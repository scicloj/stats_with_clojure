# Sample and Population

When you want to study a system, for example an issue, you can't ask everyone every question. That would require a lot of resources and if everyone should answer mandatorily, it means like you're forcing your will on people, which is not right. So statisticians go for sampling. Out of the huge population, they select some people and ask them some questions. One may answer these questions or one may not. So the people who have answered these questions are called as sample. All those people in that nation are called population. Usually the sample size is very small compared to population. Yet statisticians claim that they can predict good enough results with these samples.

## Sampling Bias

Let's take a state like Tamil Nadu in India and another state like Maharashtra. Both are highly economically developed compared to other states in India. Now, to know the lifestyle in India, if we take samples from only these two states, we'll end up being very wrong. So, sampling should be done in such a way that people from all states are considered.

There is also another bias that could be introduced. For example, Arunachal Pradesh or Meghalaya contains very less number of people compared to Bihar or Uttar Pradesh. If we take a lot of samples from Aranachal Pradesh or Meghalaya compared to Bihar or Uttar Pradesh, then our sampling is skewed too.

In order to sample, one needs to have prior knowledge of the system. And with these knowledges, one needs to carefully design how a system should be sampled.
